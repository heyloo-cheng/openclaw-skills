# Market Category Selection Guide

## Reference for Phase 5 of the Positioning Skill

Use this guide when evaluating category candidates in Phase 5. The market category is the context that makes your product's value obvious — choose wrong and even a great product looks confusing.

---

## The Four Dunford Criteria

A category must satisfy ALL four criteria to be selected:

### 1. Makes Unique Attributes Feel IMPORTANT

The category must create a context where your unique attributes (from Phase 2) are features customers actively look for — not nice-to-haves but deal-breakers.

**Test**: If a customer searching in this category saw your unique attributes listed, would they think "finally, someone who gets it" or "neat, I guess"?

**Example**:
- Category "e-book" → decision trees based on production data is a nice bonus
- Category "practitioner decision framework" → decision trees based on production data is the POINT

### 2. Sets Expectations Your Product Can EXCEED

The category sets a baseline of expectations. Your product must meet the baseline AND visibly exceed it on at least one dimension.

**Test**: What would a customer expect from a product in this category? Can you deliver all of that AND surprise them with something more?

**Trap**: Choosing a category with expectations you cannot meet. If you call it a "platform," customers expect an app. If you call it a "course," they expect video. Match your format to the category's expectations.

### 3. Is Recognized by the Target Customer

The customer must already know this category. They use this word when searching, discussing, or evaluating products.

**Test**: Would the ICP (from Phase 4) use this category name in a Google search, a Reddit post, or a conversation with a colleague? If not, you will spend marketing budget educating people about a category instead of selling a product.

**Validation methods**:
- Search volume (Google Trends, Ahrefs, or manual search)
- Community usage (search the term in the ICP's communities)
- Competitor framing (how do the Phase 1 alternatives describe themselves?)

### 4. Is NOT a Commodity Category

A commodity category is one where the customer's first question is "which one is cheapest?" This is a race to the bottom that indie builders cannot win.

**Test**: When a customer hears this category name, is their first reaction "how much?" or "tell me more"?

**Red flags**:
- Many products at low price points with no differentiation
- Customer reviews focus on price, not quality
- Amazon/Udemy saturation at the category name
- No premium tier exists in the market

## Three Category Strategies

### Head-to-Head

Position directly in an existing, well-known category.

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Customer immediately understands what you sell | You inherit all category expectations (including price ceiling) |
| No education cost | Direct comparison to established players |
| Easy to find customers searching for this category | Risk of looking like "just another X" |

**When to use**: Your unique attributes are dramatic enough to win a direct comparison. You're objectively better at the thing the customer already wants.

**Example**: "The best Terraform course" — only works if your course is genuinely, provably superior.

### Subcategory (Category + Modifier)

Take a known category and add a modifier that shifts expectations in your favor.

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Leverages existing category awareness | Modifier must be meaningful to the customer |
| Shifts competitive frame to your advantage | Can feel like marketing spin if not genuine |
| Natural price premium (specialty > generic) | Customers may ignore the modifier |

**When to use**: The existing category is too broad or competitive, but a well-chosen modifier makes your unique attributes the evaluation criteria.

**Example**: "Practitioner-grade DevOps decision framework" — customer knows "DevOps" and "decision framework," the modifier "practitioner-grade" shifts expectations toward real-world, production-tested content.

**Modifier types that work**:
- Audience-specific: "for mid-level engineers," "for solo founders"
- Methodology-specific: "practitioner-grade," "evidence-based," "production-tested"
- Format-specific: "interactive," "hands-on," "framework-based"

**Modifier types that DON'T work**:
- Generic qualifiers: "premium," "advanced," "comprehensive" (everyone claims these)
- Unverifiable claims: "best," "ultimate," "#1" (can't prove it)
- Jargon modifiers: "next-gen," "AI-powered" (unless AI is genuinely the differentiator)

### New Category

Create a category that doesn't exist yet.

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| No direct competition | Must educate market about what the category IS |
| Can define evaluation criteria | Customers can't search for a category they don't know |
| Category creator has naming advantage | Requires significant marketing investment |

**When to use**: No existing category makes your value obvious AND you have the marketing resources to educate the market. This is rare for indie builders.

**Example**: "Decision Kit" — a new category for packaged decision frameworks. Risk: no one searches for "decision kit" today.

**Rule**: Default to subcategory. New categories are seductive but expensive. If a subcategory can work, use it.

## Category Evaluation Worksheet

For each candidate category, fill out:

```
Category: _______________

1. IMPORTANCE: Does this category make my unique attributes feel important?
   □ Yes — they become the evaluation criteria
   □ Partially — they are relevant but not central
   □ No — they are nice-to-haves in this category

2. EXPECTATIONS: Can I meet AND exceed this category's expectations?
   □ Exceed — I deliver the baseline plus visible differentiators
   □ Meet — I deliver what's expected but nothing surprising
   □ Fall short — the category expects things I can't deliver

3. RECOGNITION: Does my ICP use this category name?
   □ Actively searches for it
   □ Would recognize it but doesn't search for it
   □ Would not know what this means

   Evidence: _______________

4. COMMODITY: Is this a commodity category?
   □ No — customers evaluate on quality/fit, not just price
   □ Somewhat — price matters but isn't the only factor
   □ Yes — the first question is "how much?"

5. STRATEGY: What category strategy does this represent?
   □ Head-to-head
   □ Subcategory (modifier: _______________)
   □ New category

6. VERDICT:
   □ SELECT — passes all four criteria
   □ REJECT — fails on criterion #___
   □ CONSIDER — passes but weaker than another candidate

   Reasoning: _______________
```

## Common Category Mistakes

### 1. Too Broad
"Education" or "learning" — so broad that the customer has no expectations and no search behavior.

### 2. Too Narrow
"Terraform-to-Pulumi migration decision trees for Series B startups" — so narrow that only 12 people on earth qualify.

### 3. Aspirational
Claiming a category that implies capabilities you don't have. "Platform" when you're a PDF. "Suite" when you have one tool.

### 4. Competitor's Category
Using the exact category a well-funded competitor owns. You'll always be "the other X."

### 5. Internal Jargon
Using terminology your team uses but customers don't. "DevOps decision intelligence" might make sense internally but means nothing to a mid-level engineer googling their problem.

## Validation Checklist

Before finalizing the category selection:

- [ ] Searched for the category name on Google — results are relevant (not dominated by a different meaning)
- [ ] Searched for the category name in the ICP's primary community — people use this term
- [ ] Identified at least 2 other products that exist in this category (confirms the category is real)
- [ ] Confirmed no single player dominates the category to the point of synonymy (e.g., "Kleenex" = tissue)
- [ ] Confirmed the category does NOT lead to immediate price comparison
- [ ] Confirmed the category's expected format matches your actual product format
- [ ] Confirmed the category's expected price range includes your price point
- [ ] Tested the category frame sentence: "[Product] is a [category] that [unique attribute]" — does it sound natural?
