# April Dunford's "Obviously Awesome" Positioning Framework

## Reference for the Positioning Skill

This document summarizes the positioning methodology from April Dunford's *Obviously Awesome* (2019), adapted for indie product builders operating in the hunter pipeline context. Consult this when running Phase 1-8 of the positioning skill.

---

## Core Thesis

Positioning is NOT messaging. Positioning is the context you set for your product so that its value is obvious to the people who matter most. Get the context wrong and even the best product looks confusing. Get it right and the product sells itself.

> "Positioning defines how your product is the best in the world at providing some kind of value that a well-defined set of customers cares a lot about." — April Dunford

---

## The Five Components (Dunford's Model)

Dunford identifies five components of positioning, evaluated in a specific order. The positioning skill maps these to its 8 phases:

| Dunford Component | Skill Phase | Description |
|-------------------|-------------|-------------|
| Competitive Alternatives | Phase 1 | What would customers do if your product didn't exist? |
| Unique Attributes | Phase 2 | What can you do that the alternatives can't? |
| Value | Phase 3 | What value do those unique attributes deliver? |
| Target Customer | Phase 4 | Who cares the most about that value? |
| Market Category | Phase 5 | What context makes the value obvious? |

The skill extends Dunford's model with three additional phases:
- **Phase 6: Emotional Hook Discovery** — how to make the positioning resonate emotionally
- **Phase 7: Expansion Sequence** — how messaging evolves as the product matures
- **Phase 8: Competitive Moat Identification** — which positioning advantages compound over time

---

## Why This Order Matters

Dunford's critical insight: most companies start with category ("we're a CRM") or target customer ("we're for startups") and work backward. This is wrong.

The correct order is:

1. **Start with alternatives.** What do customers use today? This grounds everything in reality.
2. **Then attributes.** What do you have that alternatives don't? This must be provable.
3. **Then value.** What does that attribute DO for the customer? Attributes ≠ value.
4. **Then customer.** Who cares MOST about that value? Not "who could use this" but "who would be desperate for this."
5. **Then category.** What context makes all of the above obvious? The category tells the customer what to expect.

If you skip steps or reverse the order, you get positioning that sounds good internally but fails in the market.

## Key Concepts

### Competitive Alternatives Are Not Competitors

Competitors are products like yours. Alternatives are what the customer actually does today. These are different.

For a decision-framework PDF, alternatives might be:
- Google searches and blog posts (DIY)
- Asking a senior engineer (internal)
- A Udemy course on the topic (indirect competitor)
- Doing nothing and making gut decisions (inaction)
- A consulting engagement (indirect competitor)

None of these are "competitors" in the traditional sense, but they are all alternatives. Your positioning must explain why you're better than THESE, not than some other PDF.

### Unique ≠ Important

An attribute can be unique but irrelevant. "Our PDF has custom illustrations" is unique but probably doesn't drive purchase decisions. "Our decision trees use real production incident data from the last 90 days" is unique AND important.

The positioning skill's Phase 2 classification (`truly_unique`, `best_in_class`, `table_stakes`, `irrelevant`) enforces this distinction. Only attributes that are both unique AND valued by the customer advance.

### Value Has Three Dimensions

| Dimension | What Changes | Evidence Source |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Functional | What can they DO? | Persona decision triggers |
| Emotional | How do they FEEL? | Persona emotional states |
| Social | How do others SEE them? | Persona social proof needs |

Most technical products focus exclusively on functional value. The emotional and social dimensions are often the actual purchase drivers.

### Market Categories Are Frames

A market category is not a description — it's a frame. It tells the customer:
- What to compare you to
- What features to expect
- How much to pay
- How to evaluate quality

Choosing the wrong category means the customer compares you to the wrong things and judges you by the wrong criteria.

**Example**: If you frame a DevOps decision kit as an "e-book," customers expect to pay $9.99 and compare you to Kindle books. If you frame it as a "practitioner decision framework," they expect to pay $29-99 and compare you to professional tools.

## Three Category Strategies

### 1. Head-to-Head (Existing Category)

Position in an existing category and win on your unique attributes.
- **When**: The category is well-known and your differentiation is clear.
- **Risk**: You inherit all category expectations, including price ceiling.
- **Example**: "A DevOps course, but built by someone who ran infra at scale, not a content creator."

### 2. Subcategory (Category + Modifier)

Take an existing category and add a modifier that highlights your unique value.
- **When**: The existing category is too broad or your differentiation is subtle.
- **Risk**: The modifier might not be meaningful to the customer.
- **Example**: "A practitioner-grade DevOps decision framework" (subcategory of "DevOps education").

### 3. New Category

Create a category that didn't exist before.
- **When**: No existing category makes your value obvious.
- **Risk**: You must educate the market about what the category even is. This is expensive and slow.
- **Example**: "Decision Kit" — a new category for packaged decision frameworks.

**Default to subcategory.** New categories are tempting but dangerous for indie builders with no marketing budget.

## Anti-Patterns

### 1. Inside-Out Positioning
Starting from what you built and trying to find who might care. Correct order: start from what customers do today.

### 2. Feature Listing
Listing features without connecting them to value. Features are attributes; you need to articulate WHY they matter.

### 3. Trying to Be for Everyone
"This is for anyone who wants to learn DevOps." This is for no one. The tighter the ICP, the stronger the positioning.

### 4. Competitor Obsession
Spending all positioning energy on why you're better than Competitor X. Customers often don't know or care about Competitor X. Position against the alternatives they ACTUALLY use.

### 5. Aspirational Category
Claiming a category you can't deliver on. If you call it a "platform" and it's a PDF, customers will feel tricked.

### 6. Commodity Framing
Choosing a category where the first customer question is "which one is cheapest?" This is a race to the bottom.

## How Positioning Feeds Downstream

### Into Pitch

| Positioning Output | Pitch Usage |
|-------------------|-------------|
| Lead message (Phase 3) | Landing page headline |
| Category frame (Phase 5) | Subheadline / positioning line |
| Primary hook (Phase 6) | Above-the-fold emotional hook |
| Anti-messaging (Phase 6) | Copy rules — terms to avoid |
| Competitive alternatives (Phase 1) | Problem section (what alternatives fail at) |
| ICP (Phase 4) | Copy targeting and channel selection |
| Expansion sequence (Phase 7) | Launch post messaging vs. follow-up messaging |
| First-conversation moats (Phase 8) | Landing page credibility claims |

### Into Landing Page

The messaging hierarchy directly determines:
- Section ordering (lead message → support → social proof → close)
- Copy register (from ICP tone analysis)
- Anti-copy rules (from anti-messaging list)

### Into Content Strategy

The expansion sequence (Phase 7) maps to the content-planner timeline:
- `now` stage messages → launch content
- `next` stage messages → month 2-6 content
- `after` stage messages → NOT content yet (internal planning only)

## Calibration: What Good Positioning Looks Like

### Good
> "Terraform Decision Kit is a practitioner decision framework that gives mid-level DevOps engineers production-ready infrastructure choices in minutes instead of days — built from real incident data, not textbook theory."

Why it works:
- Category: "practitioner decision framework" (subcategory, not commodity)
- Unique attribute: "real incident data" (technical basis, provable)
- Value: "production-ready choices in minutes instead of days" (functional + time)
- Customer: "mid-level DevOps engineers" (specific, findable)

### Bad
> "Terraform Decision Kit is a comprehensive guide to Terraform best practices for DevOps professionals."

Why it fails:
- Category: "comprehensive guide" (commodity, price ceiling ~$15)
- No unique attribute (what makes this different from blog posts?)
- No value articulation (what changes for the customer?)
- Customer: "DevOps professionals" (too broad, not findable)
